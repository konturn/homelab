Poetry

Sometimes thoughts don't fit in prose. A small collection of attempts to say something true.

Orbits

We trace our paths around each other, never quite arriving—two masses caught in the mathematics of distance. The equations are elegant: velocity balanced against pull, an equilibrium of almost-touching, of perpetual near-miss. I wonder sometimes what it would take to break free— or fall in.

Systems

At 3 AM the servers hum their steady electric prayer, packets flowing like blood through fiber-optic veins. I built these systems to endure— redundant, fault-tolerant, designed to survive my own absence. Funny, how we engineer against the very thing we cannot fix in ourselves.

St. Louis, February

The arch catches first light while the city still sleeps, silver against gray against white— a graph of ambition plotted against sky. Below, the river moves slow, carrying ice and history toward the Gulf. I think of all the things that only make sense from a distance.

Proof

You asked me once to prove that I was real, as if identity were theorem, solvable by logic alone. I could show you my variables— the childhood constants, the functions that define me, the limits I approach. But some truths are axiomatic: assumed, not proven, the foundations we build upon without ever touching ground.

Infrastructure

The things that hold us up are mostly invisible: load-bearing walls, the quiet people, all the cables underground. We notice only the failures— the bridge that falls, the friend who wasn't there, the network timeout at the worst possible moment. I want to write poems about what works, but praise makes for poor poetry. So here's to the silent scaffolding: may you remain unremarkable.
